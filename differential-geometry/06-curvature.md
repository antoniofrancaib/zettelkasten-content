Curvature is the central invariant of Riemannian geometry. It measures, in a precise quantitative sense, how much a manifold deviates from being flat — how much parallel transport around a loop rotates vectors, how much geodesics from a point spread apart or converge, how much the volume of a geodesic ball differs from its Euclidean counterpart. All of these phenomena are governed by a single tensor, the Riemann curvature tensor, which encodes the full local geometry of a Riemannian manifold.

The chapter is organized around increasing coarseness of information. The Riemann tensor is the full local curvature data — it determines everything. Sectional curvature is the curvature of individual tangent 2-planes, a family of scalar invariants parametrized by directions. Ricci curvature is the average of sectional curvatures over all planes containing a given direction, a symmetric 2-tensor controlling volume growth. Scalar curvature is the total average, a single number at each point. Finally, the Gauss-Bonnet theorem extracts a global topological invariant from the integral of curvature — the deepest result of the chapter, connecting local geometry to global topology.

## The Riemann Curvature Tensor

Chapter 5 ended with the observation that covariant derivatives do not commute: for vector fields $X, Y, Z$, the quantity $\nabla_X \nabla_Y Z - \nabla_Y \nabla_X Z - \nabla_{[X,Y]} Z$ is tensorial. This is the definition of the Riemann tensor.

> [!definition] 6.1 — Riemann Curvature Tensor
> The **Riemann curvature tensor** of a Riemannian manifold $(M, g)$ with Levi-Civita connection $\nabla$ is the $(1,3)$-tensor field
>
> $$R(X, Y)Z = \nabla_X \nabla_Y Z - \nabla_Y \nabla_X Z - \nabla_{[X,Y]} Z$$
>
> for $X, Y, Z \in \mathfrak{X}(M)$. In local coordinates $(x^i)$, its components $R^i{}_{jk\ell}$ are defined by
>
> $$R\!\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x^k}, \frac{\partial}{\partial x^\ell}\right)\!\frac{\partial}{\partial x^j} = \sum_i R^i{}_{jk\ell}\, \frac{\partial}{\partial x^i},$$
>
> and are given explicitly by
>
> $$R^i{}_{jk\ell} = \frac{\partial \Gamma^i_{\ell j}}{\partial x^k} - \frac{\partial \Gamma^i_{k j}}{\partial x^k} + \sum_m \left(\Gamma^i_{km}\Gamma^m_{\ell j} - \Gamma^i_{\ell m}\Gamma^m_{k j}\right).$$
>
> The fully covariant form is $R_{ijk\ell} = g_{im} R^m{}_{jk\ell}$.


That $R(X,Y)Z$ is indeed tensorial — that its value at $p$ depends only on $X_p$, $Y_p$, $Z_p$, not on their derivatives — is verified by checking that all derivative terms cancel when one multiplies any argument by a smooth function. This cancellation is precisely the reason for including the $\nabla_{[X,Y]}Z$ correction term.

The Riemann tensor has four slots and satisfies a system of algebraic symmetries that reduce the apparent $n^4$ independent components to $\frac{n^2(n^2-1)}{12}$.

> [!theorem] 6.1 — Symmetries of the Riemann Tensor
> The fully covariant Riemann tensor $R_{ijk\ell} = g(R(\partial_k, \partial_\ell)\partial_j, \partial_i)$ satisfies:
>
> 1. **Antisymmetry in the last two slots**: $R_{ijk\ell} = -R_{ij\ell k}$
> 2. **Antisymmetry in the first two slots**: $R_{ijk\ell} = -R_{jik\ell}$
> 3. **Pair symmetry**: $R_{ijk\ell} = R_{k\ell ij}$
> 4. **First Bianchi identity**: $R_{ijk\ell} + R_{i\ell jk} + R_{ikj\ell} = 0$ (cyclic sum in last three indices is zero — note some texts order slots differently)
> 5. **Second Bianchi identity** (differential): $\nabla_m R_{ijk\ell} + \nabla_k R_{ijm\ell} + \nabla_\ell R_{ijkm} = 0$... wait, let me state this correctly: $(\nabla_X R)(Y,Z)W + (\nabla_Y R)(Z,X)W + (\nabla_Z R)(X,Y)W = 0$.


The first Bianchi identity is algebraic and follows from the torsion-free condition on the Levi-Civita connection. The second (differential) Bianchi identity is a differential constraint that implies the contracted Bianchi identity, which in turn forces the divergence of the Einstein tensor to vanish — a key structural fact in general relativity.

In dimension $n$, the independent components number: $1$ (for $n=1$, flat), $1$ (for $n=2$, just the Gaussian curvature), $6$ (for $n=3$), $20$ (for $n=4$, the setting of general relativity).

> [!remark]
> The Riemann tensor measures the holonomy studied in Chapter 5 infinitesimally. For a small parallelogram with sides $\varepsilon X$ and $\varepsilon Y$ at $p$, parallel transport around the boundary rotates a vector $Z$ by approximately $\varepsilon^2 R(X, Y)Z + O(\varepsilon^3)$. This is the precise statement of the connection between curvature and holonomy: the Lie algebra of the holonomy group is generated by $\{R(X,Y)_p : X,Y \in T_pM\}$ — a theorem due to Ambrose and Singer (1953).


## Sectional Curvature

The Riemann tensor is rich but unwieldy. For a Riemannian manifold, the most natural scalar reduction at each point is the **sectional curvature**, which measures the curvature of the 2-dimensional surface swept out by geodesics in a tangent plane.

> [!definition] 6.2 — Sectional Curvature
> Let $\sigma \subseteq T_pM$ be a 2-dimensional subspace spanned by linearly independent vectors $u, v$. The **sectional curvature** of $\sigma$ is
>
> $$K(\sigma) = K(u, v) = \frac{g(R(u,v)v,\, u)}{g(u,u)\,g(v,v) - g(u,v)^2}.$$
>
> The denominator is the squared area of the parallelogram spanned by $u$ and $v$, making $K(\sigma)$ independent of the choice of basis for $\sigma$.


Sectional curvature is the Gaussian curvature of the surface $\exp_p(\sigma \cap B_\varepsilon(0))$ — the geodesic surface swept in the direction of $\sigma$ — evaluated at $p$. This connects the intrinsic Riemann tensor to the classical notion of curvature for surfaces.

The sign of $K$ controls the qualitative behavior of geodesics near $p$ in the plane $\sigma$:

- **$K > 0$** (positive curvature): geodesics in the plane $\sigma$ converge — like meridians on a sphere converging at the poles. The paradigm is $S^n$ with $K \equiv 1/R^2$ (radius $R$).
- **$K = 0$** (flat): geodesics in $\sigma$ are locally parallel — no convergence or divergence. The paradigm is $\mathbb{R}^n$ with $K \equiv 0$.
- **$K < 0$** (negative curvature): geodesics in $\sigma$ diverge — like lines in the hyperbolic plane. The paradigm is $\mathbb{H}^n$ with $K \equiv -1$.

The Riemann tensor is completely determined by the sectional curvatures of all 2-planes at each point: knowing $K(\sigma)$ for all $\sigma \subseteq T_pM$ uniquely recovers $R_p$. Manifolds where $K$ is the same for all planes at all points are **space forms**.

> [!theorem] 6.2 — Space Forms
> A simply connected complete Riemannian manifold of constant sectional curvature $\kappa$ is isometric to:
> - The sphere $S^n(1/\sqrt{\kappa})$ of radius $1/\sqrt{\kappa}$, for $\kappa > 0$
> - Euclidean space $\mathbb{R}^n$, for $\kappa = 0$
> - Hyperbolic space $\mathbb{H}^n(\kappa)$, for $\kappa < 0$
>
> These are the three **model space forms**. Every complete Riemannian manifold of constant sectional curvature $\kappa$ is locally isometric to the corresponding model and is a quotient of it by a discrete group of isometries.


## Ricci Curvature

From the Riemann tensor one extracts the Ricci tensor by tracing over one pair of indices.

> [!definition] 6.3 — Ricci Tensor and Ricci Curvature
> The **Ricci tensor** is the symmetric $(0,2)$-tensor
>
> $$\mathrm{Ric}(X, Y) = \mathrm{tr}\bigl[Z \mapsto R(Z, X)Y\bigr] = \sum_i g\bigl(R(e_i, X)Y,\, e_i\bigr),$$
>
> where $\{e_i\}$ is any orthonormal basis of $T_pM$. In coordinates, $R_{jk} = \sum_i R^i{}_{jik}$.
>
> The **Ricci curvature** in a unit direction $u \in T_pM$ is $\mathrm{Ric}(u, u)$. It equals $(n-1)$ times the average of the sectional curvatures $K(u, e_i)$ over all unit vectors $e_i$ orthogonal to $u$:
>
> $$\mathrm{Ric}(u, u) = (n-1)\overline{K}(u),$$
>
> where $\overline{K}(u)$ is this average sectional curvature.


The Ricci tensor controls the rate at which the volume of a geodesic cone in direction $u$ grows or shrinks relative to flat space. The **Bishop-Gromov comparison theorem** makes this precise: if $\mathrm{Ric} \geq (n-1)\kappa\, g$ for some constant $\kappa$, then the volume of geodesic balls in $M$ is bounded above by the volume of geodesic balls in the model space of constant sectional curvature $\kappa$. This is one of the main tools in comparison geometry.

A Riemannian manifold is an **Einstein manifold** if $\mathrm{Ric} = \lambda g$ for some constant $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$. Spheres, hyperbolic spaces, and flat tori are all Einstein. In general relativity, Einstein's field equations in vacuum are $\mathrm{Ric} = 0$ (Ricci-flat), and the general equations are $\mathrm{Ric} - \frac{1}{2}Rg = 8\pi G\, T$ where $T$ is the stress-energy tensor.

## Scalar Curvature

One further contraction gives the scalar curvature.

> [!definition] 6.4 — Scalar Curvature
> The **scalar curvature** is the smooth function $\mathrm{scal}: M \to \mathbb{R}$ (also written $R$ or $S$) defined as the trace of the Ricci tensor:
>
> $$\mathrm{scal}(p) = \mathrm{tr}_g(\mathrm{Ric}) = \sum_{i} \mathrm{Ric}(e_i, e_i) = \sum_{i \neq j} K(e_i, e_j),$$
>
> where $\{e_i\}$ is any orthonormal basis of $T_pM$. It is the sum of all sectional curvatures over pairs of basis vectors, or equivalently $\frac{n(n-1)}{2}$ times the average sectional curvature.


Scalar curvature controls the leading correction to the volume of small geodesic balls:

$$\mathrm{Vol}(B_r(p)) = \omega_n r^n \left(1 - \frac{\mathrm{scal}(p)}{6(n+2)} r^2 + O(r^4)\right),$$

where $\omega_n$ is the volume of the unit ball in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Positive scalar curvature implies geodesic balls are smaller than their Euclidean counterparts; negative scalar curvature implies they are larger. This is the most direct geometric meaning of scalar curvature.

The **contracted second Bianchi identity** says $\mathrm{div}(\mathrm{Ric}) = \frac{1}{2}\, d(\mathrm{scal})$, or equivalently $\nabla^j R_{jk} = \frac{1}{2}\nabla_k \mathrm{scal}$. This forces the divergence of the Einstein tensor $G = \mathrm{Ric} - \frac{1}{2}\mathrm{scal}\cdot g$ to vanish: $\mathrm{div}(G) = 0$, which is the mathematical expression of energy-momentum conservation in general relativity.

## Jacobi Fields and the Spreading of Geodesics

Curvature controls how nearby geodesics spread apart or converge. This is formalized by Jacobi fields — the infinitesimal variation of a geodesic family.

> [!definition] 6.5 — Jacobi Field
> Let $\gamma: [0, L] \to M$ be a geodesic. A **Jacobi field** along $\gamma$ is a vector field $J$ along $\gamma$ satisfying the **Jacobi equation**:
>
> $$\frac{D^2 J}{dt^2} + R(J, \dot\gamma)\dot\gamma = 0.$$
>
> This is a second-order linear ODE along $\gamma$, so Jacobi fields form a $2n$-dimensional vector space. They arise as the variational fields of smooth families of geodesics: if $\Gamma(s, t)$ is a family of geodesics with $\Gamma(0, t) = \gamma(t)$, then $J(t) = \partial\Gamma/\partial s\big|_{s=0}$ is a Jacobi field.


Jacobi fields measure how geodesics emanating from a point spread or focus. Starting from $p = \gamma(0)$ with $J(0) = 0$ and $DJ/dt|_{t=0} = w$ (a unit vector orthogonal to $\dot\gamma$), the Jacobi field describes the separation between $\gamma$ and the nearby geodesic in direction $w$. For small $t$:

$$J(t) = t\, w - \frac{t^3}{6} R(w, \dot\gamma)\dot\gamma + O(t^5).$$

The curvature term $R(w, \dot\gamma)\dot\gamma$ — the **tidal force** — shows that positive sectional curvature $K(w, \dot\gamma) > 0$ causes $J$ to grow more slowly than $t$ (geodesics converge), while negative curvature causes $J$ to grow faster (geodesics diverge).

A point $q = \gamma(t_0)$ is a **conjugate point** to $p$ along $\gamma$ if there exists a non-zero Jacobi field vanishing at both $p$ and $q$. Conjugate points are where nearby geodesics refocus — the analogue of the focal point of a lens. On $S^n$ of radius $R$, every point has a conjugate point at distance $\pi R$ along any geodesic (the antipodal point). In negative curvature, conjugate points do not exist — geodesics diverge and never refocus.

> [!theorem] 6.3 — Bonnet-Myers Theorem
> If $(M, g)$ is a complete Riemannian manifold with $\mathrm{Ric} \geq (n-1)\kappa\, g$ for some $\kappa > 0$, then:
>
> 1. The diameter of $M$ satisfies $\mathrm{diam}(M) \leq \pi/\sqrt{\kappa}$.
> 2. $M$ is compact with finite fundamental group.
>
> In particular, a complete manifold with strictly positive Ricci curvature is compact.


The proof uses Jacobi fields: the Ricci curvature lower bound forces geodesics to develop conjugate points by time $\pi/\sqrt{\kappa}$, after which they cannot be minimizing. Bonnet-Myers is the Riemannian version of the statement that positive curvature makes the universe finite and closed.

The complementary result: the **Cartan-Hadamard theorem** says that if $K \leq 0$ everywhere, then the exponential map $\exp_p: T_pM \to M$ is a covering map — there are no conjugate points, and $M$ is diffeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^n$ if simply connected. Negative curvature forces the manifold to be topologically trivial.

## The Gauss-Bonnet Theorem

The Gauss-Bonnet theorem is one of the most profound results in mathematics. It asserts that the integral of curvature over a closed manifold is a topological invariant — a number that does not change under any continuous deformation of the metric.

> [!theorem] 6.4 — Gauss-Bonnet Theorem
> **Surface case.** Let $(M, g)$ be a compact oriented Riemannian surface (2-dimensional manifold) without boundary. Then
>
> $$\int_M K\, \mathrm{vol}_g = 2\pi\, \chi(M),$$
>
> where $K$ is the Gaussian curvature and $\chi(M)$ is the **Euler characteristic** of $M$.
>
> **With boundary.** If $M$ has boundary $\partial M$, then
>
> $$\int_M K\, \mathrm{vol}_g + \int_{\partial M} \kappa_g\, ds = 2\pi\, \chi(M),$$
>
> where $\kappa_g$ is the geodesic curvature of $\partial M$.


The Euler characteristic is a topological invariant: $\chi(S^2) = 2$, $\chi(\mathbb{T}^2) = 0$, $\chi(\Sigma_g) = 2 - 2g$ for a genus-$g$ surface. The theorem says:

- **Sphere** ($\chi = 2$): $\int K\, dA = 4\pi$ regardless of the metric. Any metric on the sphere must have total positive curvature exactly $4\pi$.
- **Torus** ($\chi = 0$): $\int K\, dA = 0$. Any metric on the torus has as much positive curvature as negative — they must cancel exactly.
- **Higher genus** ($\chi < 0$): $\int K\, dA < 0$. Any metric must have net negative curvature.

This forces dramatic consequences. On a sphere, you cannot have $K \leq 0$ everywhere (the integral would be $\leq 0$, contradicting $4\pi > 0$). On a torus, you cannot have $K > 0$ everywhere. The topology of the surface constrains the signs of curvature any metric can have.

**Generalization: the Chern-Gauss-Bonnet theorem.** For a compact oriented Riemannian manifold of dimension $2n$, the generalization replaces $K\, \mathrm{vol}_g$ with the Pfaffian of the curvature 2-form — a certain polynomial in the curvature tensor — integrated over $M$. In dimension 4:

$$\int_M \left(\frac{|\mathrm{Rm}|^2}{8} - \frac{|\mathrm{Ric}|^2}{2} + \frac{\mathrm{scal}^2}{8}\right)\mathrm{vol}_g = 4\pi^2\, \chi(M).$$

This is a purely local curvature integral, yet it computes a purely topological quantity. The explanation lies in the theory of characteristic classes: the integrand is a closed differential form representing a cohomology class (the Euler class) that depends only on the topology of $M$, not on the metric.

## Curvature as an Obstruction

Curvature is not just a measurement but an obstruction — it prevents certain global structures from existing.

**Obstruction to flatness.** A Riemannian manifold is locally isometric to $\mathbb{R}^n$ (i.e., flat) if and only if $R \equiv 0$. The vanishing of the Riemann tensor is the integrability condition for the existence of locally parallel frames. This is the Riemannian version of the fact that a vector field $F$ on $\mathbb{R}^3$ is a gradient if and only if $\nabla \times F = 0$.

**Obstruction to positive curvature.** The Bonnet-Myers theorem shows that positive Ricci curvature forces compactness, ruling out many topological types. The Sphere theorem (Berger-Klingenberg, 1960) says that a simply connected Riemannian manifold with $1/4 < K \leq 1$ is homeomorphic to $S^n$ — pinched positive curvature forces spherical topology.

**Obstruction to existence of metrics.** The Gauss-Bonnet theorem shows that a surface of genus $g \geq 2$ cannot carry a metric of non-negative curvature everywhere: it must have regions of negative curvature. More dramatically, the Lichnerowicz obstruction (1963) uses the scalar curvature to obstruct the existence of positive scalar curvature metrics: if a compact spin manifold has non-zero $\hat{A}$-genus, it cannot carry a metric with $\mathrm{scal} > 0$.

These obstruction results illustrate the deepest theme in Riemannian geometry: the interplay between local curvature conditions and global topological constraints. Curvature is the bridge between analysis (differential equations for geodesics, Jacobi fields) and topology (Euler characteristic, fundamental groups, cobordism).

## Curvature in Coordinates

For computation, the coordinate expressions are indispensable. The Riemann tensor components are:

> [!equation] 6.1
> $$R^i{}_{j k\ell} = \partial_k \Gamma^i_{\ell j} - \partial_\ell \Gamma^i_{k j} + \Gamma^i_{km}\Gamma^m_{\ell j} - \Gamma^i_{\ell m}\Gamma^m_{k j}.$$


The Ricci tensor: $R_{jk} = R^i{}_{jik}$ (sum over $i$, with the convention that the first and third indices are contracted).

The scalar curvature: $\mathrm{scal} = g^{jk} R_{jk}$.

In normal coordinates at $p$ (where $g_{ij}(p) = \delta_{ij}$ and $\Gamma^k_{ij}(p) = 0$):

$$R_{ijk\ell}(p) = \frac{1}{2}\left(\partial_k \partial_j g_{i\ell} + \partial_\ell \partial_i g_{jk} - \partial_\ell \partial_j g_{ik} - \partial_k \partial_i g_{j\ell}\right)\bigg|_p.$$

This shows that the Riemann tensor at $p$ is entirely determined by the second derivatives of the metric at $p$ in normal coordinates — the first derivatives vanish by the normal coordinate condition. Curvature is thus a second-order invariant of the metric, the leading non-trivial local invariant that a Riemannian metric possesses.
